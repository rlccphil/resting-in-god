Resting in God

Silence, Solitude, and Recovering from Ministry Fatigue

"Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest." Matthew 11:28
Part One · 15 Minutes

Opening Stillness & Scripture

Arriving spiritually, not just logging in mentally.

We began our retreat not with information, but with an invitation. Before the teaching, before the questions, before the breakout rooms — we were asked simply to arrive. To bring our whole selves, weary and all, into the presence of God.

Our facilitator set the tone with a gentle but important word: "This is not a class to perform in, but a space to rest in God." That sentence matters. For those of us in ministry, performance can become instinctive — we show up ready to contribute, ready to be seen as engaged. But this retreat asked something different of us.

✦ The Breath Prayer ✦

Inhale slowly… "Come to me…"
Exhale slowly… "I will give you rest…"

Take a moment now to breathe this prayer. Slowly. Deliberately. Let these words drop below your mind and into your chest.

"Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light."

— Matthew 11:28–30 (ESV)

We read this passage twice — slowly, in the tradition of Lectio Divina. The goal was not to analyze it, but to receive it. We were then asked: "What word or phrase is God highlighting to you?"

Perhaps it was the word come. Or the phrase heavy laden. Or the promise you will find rest for your souls. Whatever it was, that word became your entry point into the rest of our retreat together.

The retreat begins not when you have settled your thoughts, but when you have given God permission to meet you exactly where you are.

We closed the opening with five minutes of silent reflection — sitting quietly with the word or phrase that surfaced. Not to solve anything. Not to figure out what it means yet. Simply to be still, and let God be God.

· · ✦ · ·

🔁 Revisit This Practice

You can return to this opening practice at any time — not just during a formal retreat. Try it this week:

  1. 1Find a quiet spot. Take three slow breaths.
  2. 2Read Matthew 11:28–30 once, slowly.
  3. 3Ask: What word or phrase is standing out to me today?
  4. 4Sit in silence with that phrase for 3–5 minutes.
  5. 5Write what you sense, if anything, before moving on with your day.
Part Two · 45 Minutes

The Rest We Resist

Ministry fatigue is not solved by better strategy, but by deeper surrender to Jesus.

The teaching began by naming something most ministry leaders rarely say out loud: we are tired. Not just physically tired from a demanding week. Many of us carry a tiredness that sleep does not fix — a fatigue that is layered, complex, and often hidden beneath our public ministry persona.

Part A · 10 Minutes

The Reality of Ministry Fatigue

We were gently asked to name our experience without minimizing it. Ministry fatigue often shows up in these forms:

  • Chronic tiredness — a baseline weariness that doesn't lift
  • Emotional depletion — giving to others until there is nothing left for yourself or your family
  • Hidden discouragement — disappointed in ministry results, disappointed in people, or disappointed in yourself
  • Performing instead of pastoring — going through the motions because the genuine love and energy have drained away

"Many of us are not just tired — we are carrying more than Jesus asked us to carry."

This is the crucial diagnostic. Jesus addresses in Matthew 11 those who are "heavy laden" — and for many pastors, the heaviness is not just the work itself. It is the weight of unasked expectations, identity entanglement, and self-reliance layered on top of the work.

Part B · 15 Minutes

The Invitation of Jesus: Three Movements

The text offers three distinct and progressive invitations. Each is worth sitting with carefully:

1. "Come to Me" → Relational, not operational

Jesus does not say, "Come to a method." He doesn't say, "Come to a renewal program." He says, Come to Me — to a Person. Rest begins not with stopping work, but with coming to Jesus. This is the Reformational heart of spiritual renewal: it is always about relationship before technique.

2. "Take My Yoke" → Shared life, not solo burden

A yoke in the first-century context was used for two animals working together — the stronger guiding the weaker. Jesus is not saying remove all burden. He is saying, let's carry it together. Notice the contrast:

OUR YOKE

  • Driven by pressure
  • Motivated by expectation
  • Rooted in self-reliance
  • Heavy & isolating

HIS YOKE

  • Carried in grace
  • Aligned with God's call
  • Shared with companionship
  • Easy & light

3. "You Will Find Rest for Your Souls" → Deep rest

This is not vacation rest. Not physical rest (though we need that too). This is soul-level rest — an inner quietness that persists even when ministry is demanding. It is freedom from striving, from needing to prove, from anxious self-monitoring. This is the rest the world cannot offer and the self cannot manufacture. It must be found — in Jesus.

Part C · 10 Minutes

Why We Struggle to Rest

If rest is Jesus's gift and invitation, why do we resist it? The teaching named several heart-level issues worth honest examination:

  • Identity tied to ministry output — "If I am not producing, I am not valuable."
  • Fear of irrelevance — "If I slow down, I will be forgotten or replaced."
  • Messiah complex — "If I don't do this, it won't get done."
  • Inability to be still — Silence feels threatening when we have been conditioned to equate busyness with faithfulness.

"What are you afraid will happen if you slow down?"

— A question worth sitting with
Part D · 10 Minutes

The Way of Jesus: Gentle and Lowly

Perhaps the most healing part of the teaching was this: Jesus is not harsh with tired leaders.

"For I am gentle and lowly in heart…" (v.29) — this is the only place in all four Gospels where Jesus explicitly describes His own heart. And what He offers is not a demanding master disappointed with underperformance. He is gentle. He is lowly — which means He comes down to where you are.

"The One you are coming to is not demanding more — He is offering rest."

This is the theological ground of our retreat. We can rest because the One who invites us to rest is safe. He knows our frame. He remembers that we are dust (Psalm 103:14). He is not standing over us with a performance review — He is standing before us with an open invitation.

Part Three · 30 Minutes

Guided Silence & Solitude

"Now we move from talking about rest… to entering it."

This is where the retreat moved from content to encounter. After the teaching, we were invited to set aside words — our own and others' — and simply be present with God. For many of us, this is the most unfamiliar and sometimes the most uncomfortable part of a retreat.

The structure was intentional, guiding us gently into deeper quiet rather than throwing us into the deep end of silence.

📋 The Structure of Our Silence

  1. Prepare We were asked to turn off cameras if helpful, sit comfortably, set aside phones, and have a journal nearby. No multitasking. Full presence.
  2. Enter Our guide led us slowly through a visualization: "Picture yourself coming to Jesus… You are carrying something heavy… What is it?" — then a pause — "Now hear Him say: 'Come to me…'"
  3. Silence We were given 15–18 minutes of unstructured quiet, with three questions to hold (not answer analytically, but sit with prayerfully).
  4. Return A gentle invitation back: a deep breath, then writing anything we sensed or received.

Silence is not the absence of God. It is often the clearest space in which to hear Him.

The Three Questions of Silence

These were the prompts displayed during our silent time. They are not meant to be answered in sequence like a worksheet, but held prayerfully — like open palms rather than clenched fists:

  • 🌿 What am I carrying right now?
  • 🌿 What is Jesus inviting me to release?
  • 🌿 What does it mean for me to take His yoke?

You may wish to return to this practice on your own. Even 10–15 minutes of intentional silence — not as spiritual performance, but as simple attentiveness — can become a lifeline in a demanding ministry season. The Desert Fathers called this hesychia: the inner stillness that is not emptiness, but the fullness of God's presence in quiet.

✦ Try It Now ✦

Set aside 5 minutes. Use the breath prayer to settle.
Then simply ask: What am I carrying right now?
Let whatever surfaces, surface. Don't fix it. Bring it to Jesus.

Part Four · 60 Minutes

Spiritual Conversations

Sharing what surfaced — in triads, with sacred attentiveness.

After silence, we moved into one of the most powerful elements of the retreat: small-group spiritual conversation. In triads, we were given the gift of being truly heard — not analyzed, not counseled, not evaluated. Simply heard, with prayerful attention.

This is a different kind of conversation than we typically practice in ministry settings. It requires a particular posture — one that many of us had to be intentionally reminded of.

The Guidelines We Were Given

  • 👂 Listen, don't fix. Our role in this space is not to solve each other's ministry problems. It is to listen with full presence.
  • 🚫 No advice unless asked. Even well-intentioned advice can short-circuit the deeper work God is doing in someone. Hold back the impulse to help with solutions.
  • 🔒 Honor confidentiality. What is shared in the triad stays in the triad. This is sacred trust.
  • 🙏 Pay attention to God, not just the story. As someone shares, you are not just listening to their narrative — you are listening for where God might be at work in it.

📋 The Sharing Flow

Each person took approximately 15–18 minutes, sharing around three questions:

  1. 1 What surfaced during our time of silence?
  2. 2 Where do you feel weary right now?
  3. 3 What might Jesus be inviting you into?

How Listeners Responded

After each person shared, the others responded only in these two ways — nothing more:

💬 "What I hear God might be doing in you is…"

🙏 A short, unhurried prayer for the person.

This restraint is not coldness — it is reverence. We are witnesses to the Spirit's work, not managers of it.

To be truly listened to — without being analyzed or advised — is itself a form of grace. It mirrors the way God receives us.

This kind of spiritual conversation is one of the most underutilized gifts available to ministry leaders. We rarely give ourselves or each other the experience of being genuinely heard. These conversations reminded us that we are not alone in our fatigue — and that the Spirit is at work not despite our weakness, but through our willingness to name it honestly.

Part Five · 15 Minutes

The Examen & Listening Prayer

A simplified Ignatian review of the day and season.

We closed the substantive portion of the retreat with a time of structured reflection called the Examen — a practice rooted in Ignatian spirituality, adapted here for ministry leaders in a season of fatigue. Unlike journaling or note-taking, the Examen is not analytical. It is a prayerful review, an attentive listening for where God has been present in our experience.

The facilitator led us slowly through four movements. Each is a gentle question — not a demand for a full answer, but an invitation to notice:

1

Gratitude

"What are you thankful for in this season?"
Begin not with problems, but with gifts. Even in fatigue, there are signs of grace. What has God given you — in relationships, in moments of unexpected joy, in glimpses of His faithfulness — that you can name with thanks?

2

Awareness

"Where have you felt most drained?"
This is not a complaint session — it is honest acknowledgment. God does not ask us to pretend we are well when we are weary. Where have you felt the most depleted? What has been heaviest to carry? Name it with honesty before the One who already knows.

3

Invitation

"Where is Jesus inviting you to rest?"
Not just rest in general — but specifically, in your life and ministry right now. Is He inviting you to slow a particular pace? To release a particular burden? To receive a particular grace? Listen quietly for a few moments before answering.

4

Response

"What is one small step of surrender?"
Not a strategy. Not a ministry plan. One small act of yielding to Jesus — this week, in your specific context. It could be canceling one meeting to simply pray. It could be saying "no" to one commitment. It could be asking a trusted friend to pray for you. What is the one small step?

The Examen is not about reviewing your performance. It is about reviewing God's faithfulness — and your response to His invitation.

The Examen is most powerful when done regularly — not just in a retreat setting. Many spiritual directors recommend a brief version (10–15 minutes) at the close of each day, or at least weekly. Over time, it trains us to see our lives through the lens of God's presence rather than our own self-assessment.

"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting."

— Psalm 139:23–24
Part Six · 15 Minutes

Closing Prayer & Blessing

Sent from rest — not running toward it.

We did not end the retreat with a challenge or a call to action. We ended with a blessing. This itself is a counter-cultural act in ministry formation: to close not with "now go do more," but with "you are seen, you are loved, you are sent."

The facilitator prayed over us corporately, then spoke the promise of Matthew 11 over us slowly — as a benediction, not a command:

✦ The Prayer Prayed Over Us ✦

"Lord Jesus,
You see Your servants…
Not as machines, but as beloved…
Not as workers who have fallen short,
But as children who have labored long.
Restore what has been depleted.
Quiet what striving has stirred.
And let them find in You
the rest that only You can give."

"…and you will find rest for your souls."

— Matthew 11:29b

This verse — spoken over us as a benediction — is not a past tense statement. It is a living promise in the present tense: you will find. The rest is available. The invitation stands. Jesus is not withdrawing the offer because the retreat is over.

You do not leave this retreat and return to life unaided. You leave carrying the same Jesus who invited you in.

Carrying This Forward

As you return to your ministry context, consider anchoring the retreat's fruit in these simple habits:

  • A daily breath prayer — even 3 minutes of intentional silence with Matthew 11:28 as your anchor.
  • A weekly Examen — reviewing the week through the lens of gratitude, awareness, invitation, and response.
  • A trusted spiritual conversation partner — someone with whom you can practice the kind of listening we experienced in the triad. Not advice-giving, but prayerful, attentive presence.
  • Permission to receive — you give much in ministry. Practice also receiving: grace, care, rest, love. The yoke is not yours alone.

"He gives power to the faint, and to him who has no might he increases strength. Even youths shall faint and be weary, and young men shall fall exhausted; but they who wait for the Lord shall renew their strength; they shall mount up with wings like eagles…"

— Isaiah 40:29–31

May this not be the end of a retreat, but the beginning of a new rhythm — one shaped less by the pace of ministry demands, and more by the gentle, lowly heart of Jesus.
